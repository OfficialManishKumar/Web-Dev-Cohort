Origin of the Internet

Imagine the 1940s-1950s when there was no Internet, no social media, no blogs, no articles, and no way to talk with friends online. Computers existed but were enormous - imagine a computer the size of a giant mammoth or three elephants! The Story of the Internet

The First Network: ARPANET (1969)

In 1969, a group of brilliant researchers at ARPA (Advanced Research Projects Agency, a major science funding organization) decided to make computers talk to each other. They built ARPANET, the first computer network connecting four American universities. Two key figures, Frank Heart and Leonard Kleinrock, set up special boxes called IMPs (Interface Message Processors) to send packets of data.

The first message ever sent through the Internet was simply "LO." They had actually intended to send "LOGIN," but the computer crashed after just two letters! Despite this hiccup, it was a tremendous victory because it was the first time a message was sent through the Internet without using traditional long cables.


How Do 0s and 1s Travel in the Open Environment?

Let's understand this concept with an example. Imagine you've got your crush's number and you message "Hi" to them. Here's how this message reaches your crush (I'll use some technical terms like TCP or IP, but don't worry, I'll explain them shortly):

  1. Binary Step:

    First, your message is converted into 0s and 1s. For example, "H" becomes "01001000" and "i" becomes "01101001". Similarly, all letters are converted into binary code.

  2. Packet Step:

    Second, these 0s and 1s are organized into packets and guided by protocols like TCP/IP. Think of these protocols as sets of rules. We convert binary codes into packets because messages can be too long and difficult to transport in one piece, so we break them into smaller "envelopes" called packets.

  3. Signal Step:

    Third, your message is converted into either electrical signals (if using cables) or radio/light signals (if sharing data wirelessly).

  4. Destination and Decoding:

    Finally, the packets arrive at the server where the 0s and 1s are transformed back into readable data.

  5. Delivery:

    The same process repeats, and your message is received and decrypted by your crush's phone.

In this process, there are several helpers:

  • Encoder:

    It turns 0s and 1s into packets, like packing a letter.

  • Cables and Antennas:

    These send signals through physical cables or through the air.

  • TCP and IP:

    These protocols break data into packets and guide them, like a postman with a map that shows where each packet needs to go.

  • Decoder:

    This turns signals back into 0s and 1s, which are then converted into readable text.

And all of this happens in milliseconds!


Technical Jargon Explained

Whenever you try to learn about the Internet, programming, or web development, you'll encounter these technical terms. When you search for them online, the explanations can be complex enough to make you skip these topics altogether. But not anymore! Let's discuss the most common terms in the simplest way possible.

  • User Agent

    This is an agent that requests information from a server on your behalf. Don't get confused - it's generally your browser, like Chrome or Firefox.

  • TCP

    Transmission Control Protocol. Breaking it down: "Transmission" means "Transfer," "Control" means "controls," and "Protocol" means "set of rules." It's simply the set of rules followed during data transfer.

    When we send data, it's transported in packets from the client (browser/you/user) to the server. During this process, there's a chance that some packets might not reach their destination or arrive in the wrong sequence, which could corrupt the data. TCP ensures that all packets arrive and, if any are missing, it requests them again so that no information is lost. TCP also arranges the packets in the correct order so the data remains intact.

    The downside of TCP is that it takes more time to arrange and re-request missing packets, which can be problematic for time-sensitive applications like live video streaming, messaging, or online gaming.

  • DNS

    Domain Name System (also called DNS server or DNS system). Think of it like your phone's contact list. You probably don't memorize all your contacts' phone numbers - instead, you save their contact information with a name. When you need to call someone, you search for their name in your contacts and get their number.

    Similarly, with websites, the contact information is the IP address of your requested domain (like https://www.google.com). The "contacts" in this case is the "DNS server," which gives you the IP address, and the "browser" is the "requester" because it asks for the IP address on your behalf.

  • IP

    Internet Protocol. It's like your phone number or address for the internet. It's a rulebook (protocol) that helps devices (like computers and phones) communicate and find each other. Every device gets a unique IP address, similar to your phone or house number. It also helps find the best and nearest path to reach the destination. IP addresses typically look like "192.168.1.1."

Here are some more technical terms that might sound complex but are actually quite simple:

Term Explanation
Payload The main content or actual data being transferred
Cache Temporarily stored data that helps websites load faster on repeat visits
Bandwidth The amount of data that can travel through an internet connection at once, like the width of a road for data packets
Router A device that directs (or transfers) data packets between devices and networks or the Internet, like a traffic officer for data
Cloud A network of servers storing and processing data over the Internet
Firewall A security system that blocks unwanted data or hackers from entering your device or network, like a gatekeeper
Latency The time taken for data to travel from one place to another, similar to a delay in delivery
